Outreach Activities

Local governments are changing. Forward-thinking citizens and public servants across the country are leveraging technology to innovate the way our cities work — and it’s accelerated by an exchange of ideas, solutions, best practices, and even software code between like-minded leaders.

In 2013 CfA led this conversation through our programs, annual conference, a published book and online content, and collaborative tech projects. We exposed city officials to ideas like lean thinking and user-centered design - and watched them take it back and change they way they work. Cities are now appointing Chief Data Officers and Chief Innovation Officers; they are redeploying each other’s apps; and they are working together to take on common policy challenges.

The Code for America Summit

In its third year the Summit retained an intimate community feeling, but with even more energy and momentum. Over 500+ people participated with over 80 cities and counties represented, along with folks from our partners in 7 other countries. The sentiment was widely felt at this October gathering that Code for America’s mission to connect citizens and cities to help government work better with technology has turned into a broad movement.

Beyond Transparency:
A Book on Open Data Best Practices

A joint project co-edited by Chicago’s former Chief Data Officer, Brett Goldstein, and CfA Content Manager Lauren Dyson, Beyond Transparency is a cross-disciplinary survey of the open data landscape featuring practitioners sharing their own stories of what they’ve accomplished with open civic data. The book seeks to move beyond the rhetoric of transparency for transparency’s sake and towards action and problem solving.

Civic Data Standards:
Putting Data in the Hands of Citizens

Governments gather massive amounts of valuable data. But data is only useful when it's made available to the public, and integrated with the apps and websites people already use everyday. This year Code for America led the collaborative creation of 3 new civic data standards. One of these, LIVES, made restaurant inspection data readily available to diners -- by putting it on Yelp.
